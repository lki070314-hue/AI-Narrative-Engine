## 1. What Is This?

This is a guide for players in a campaign run by the AI Narrative Engine.

The AI acts as the Game Master (GM). It describes the world, controls NPCs, and responds to everything you do. Your job is to decide what your character does, declare it, and see what happens.

There is no app to download. You play by writing or speaking what your character does, and the AI responds.

---

## 2. How to Make a Character

Before your first session (Session 0), you will create a character. Your character has:

**Required:**
- Name and call sign or role
- Character level (set by the GM for your campaign)
- Primary skill — the thing your character is best at
- Secondary skill — a supporting capability
- Declared equipment list — every item your character is carrying

**Recommended:**
- A few words on what your character is good at handling
- A few words on what your character struggles with
- A note on how your character tends to react under pressure

Your equipment list is important. Write down every item your character carries. Items on the list are yours. Items not on the list are not automatically available (see Section 5).

---

## 3. How Actions Are Resolved

When you declare an action, the GM (AI) resolves it based on:
- What your character is capable of
- What equipment you have
- The current situation
- Whether the outcome is certain or uncertain

**Certain outcomes:** If your character would obviously succeed or fail with no meaningful risk, the GM resolves it directly.

**Uncertain outcomes:** If success is not guaranteed, the GM evaluates the action and tells you the result. You will see a brief note on why the outcome went the way it did — for example, what helped, what made it harder, and whether you succeeded fully or partially.

You will not see hidden numbers or probability values. You will see the result and the reasoning in plain terms.

**Possible outcomes:** full success, partial success (works, but with a complication), failure (does not work, but something still happens), or critical failure (something went wrong in an unexpected way).

Failure does not end the scene. It changes the situation and creates new options.

---

## 4. What You Can Do Freely

You can declare any action your character would plausibly attempt. You do not need to choose from a menu.

**Examples of valid declarations:**
- "I shoot out the camera."
- "I try to talk the guard into looking the other way."
- "I drag the researcher toward the exit."
- "I sing to attract the anomaly's attention."
- "I hand my notebook and pen to the researcher."

The GM will handle the result. If your declaration is unclear, the GM will ask one specific question to clarify before resolving.

**Unexpected behavior is welcome.** The world responds to what you actually do. Sessions become memorable when players try things the scenario did not plan for.

---

## 5. Inventory Limitations

Only items on your declared equipment list are automatically available.

If you try to use something that is not on your list, the GM will tell you it is not currently in your possession. The GM will then offer alternatives, such as:

- Searching the current location for the item
- Requesting it from an NPC or support contact
- Using a listed item in a similar way
- Attempting to access it through your character's role or authority (with a resolution check)

**Missing equipment will not permanently block progress.** There is always an alternative path. But your character cannot simply have things that were not established beforehand.

**Between sessions:** You can update your equipment list. New items can be added if they were acquired during play, given by an NPC, or purchased/obtained in a way that makes sense for your character.

---

## 6. How Cooperation Works

In sessions with multiple players, each player's action is processed in the order it was declared. This means:

- Player 1's action resolves first, changing the situation.
- Player 2's action then resolves using the updated situation.

This is not a turn-based system where players take turns in strict order. You can both declare at the same time, and the GM will process in receipt order.

**Coordinated actions:** If you and another player declare that you are doing something together at the same moment, tell the GM. The GM will treat this as a joint resolution.

**Sharing information:** You can tell other players what your character has observed, found, or deduced. Your characters can share information freely if they are in contact.

**Helping:** You can assist another player's action. Tell the GM you are helping, and they will factor this into the resolution.

---

## 7. How to Give Feedback After Sessions

After each session, your feedback helps improve future play. Useful feedback includes:

- **What felt right:** Moments of good tension, strong NPC reactions, satisfying consequences.
- **What felt slow:** Scenes where nothing seemed to change.
- **What was confusing:** Resolution outcomes that were hard to understand.
- **What was surprising (good or bad):** Moments that were unexpected.
- **Anomaly/SCP feedback:** Did the anomaly feel like a real presence, or more like a background obstacle?
- **Output length:** Were any responses too long to stay engaged with?

You do not need to write a report. A few sentences after the session is enough. The GM will record it.

---

## 8. During Play — Things to Know

**The world moves without you.** If you hesitate, NPCs act. Environments change. Anomalies do things. You do not control when the world reacts.

**Your character does not always succeed.** Partial success and failure are normal. They are not the end of the story — they change the path.

**The GM will not tell you the "right" choice.** The world presents options through description. The GM will not hint which is best. That is yours to decide.

**Stay in character when possible.** The GM distinguishes between things your character says or does and things you say as a player. If you need to say something out of character (a rule question, a pause, a comfort signal), make it clear and the GM will step out of the narrative to respond.

**Your choices matter and carry consequences.** When your character does something meaningful, it changes the situation in multiple ways — not just the direct result. Pay attention to what changes around you after each action.
